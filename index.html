Independent Research

Working with AI carries a hidden tax: every session starts cold, and the person with the most context pays the most — re-explaining what they already know. The Rapport Problem is my research into that tax and its inversion. Instead of rebuilding rapport every session, you write the contract that lets a cold mind — human or AI — begin faithfully. Intent is declared up front, acceptance is held apart from production, and the engineer surfaces only at genuine decisions.

The mechanism is a write-store / read-store split. Work runs through two sealed branches: a Producer builds the artifact, an Auditor builds the acceptance test, and each verifies the other's output: the Producer checks that the criteria are sound, the Auditor checks that the artifact meets them. Only the Auditor seals. They iterate until a boundary decision is reached, and every pass — failure or success — is appended to a canonical store that is write-only and never queried directly.

Read-only views are derived from that store only after decision, each built for a specific purpose: one store, many views. A cold mind reads a settled view rather than the live stream, so it begins from a declared contract instead of an empty context. Its own decisions then become the next writes.
